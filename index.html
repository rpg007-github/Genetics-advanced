Section 01
Epigenetics & Chromatin Biology

Heritable changes in gene expression that don't alter the DNA sequence itself.

Chromatin Structure & the Histone Code

DNA wraps around histone octamers (2 each of H2A, H2B, H3, H4) to form nucleosomes, the basic unit of chromatin. H1 is the linker histone. The N-terminal tails of histones extend outward and are targets for covalent modifications that regulate gene access.

Euchromatin (loosely packed, transcriptionally active) vs. heterochromatin (tightly packed, transcriptionally silent). Constitutive heterochromatin (centromeres, telomeres) is always condensed; facultative heterochromatin (e.g., the inactive X) can switch states.

Key Concept
The histone code hypothesis: specific combinations of histone modifications are read by effector proteins to determine transcriptional state. Writers (add marks), readers (recognize marks), and erasers (remove marks) form the regulatory machinery.

DNA Methylation

Addition of a methyl group to the 5-carbon of cytosine, primarily at CpG dinucleotides. Catalyzed by DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs): DNMT1 maintains methylation after replication; DNMT3a/3b establish new methylation patterns.

Methylation at promoter CpG islands generally silences transcription by blocking transcription factor binding and recruiting methyl-CpG binding proteins (MeCP2) that attract HDACs.

Clinical Connection
Rett syndrome results from mutations in MECP2, which reads DNA methylation marks. Aberrant DNA methylation is a hallmark of cancer (global hypomethylation + promoter hypermethylation of tumor suppressors).

Genomic Imprinting

Parent-of-origin-specific gene expression: one allele is epigenetically silenced by DNA methylation established in the germline. Only the maternal or paternal allele is expressed.

Imprinting control regions (ICRs) are differentially methylated. Imprinting is reset each generation in the germline.

Examples
Prader-Willi syndrome: loss of paternal expression at 15q11-13. Angelman syndrome: loss of maternal UBE3A expression at the same region. Same chromosomal deletion, different syndrome depending on parent of origin.

X-Inactivation in Depth

In female mammals, one X is silenced by the long non-coding RNA XIST, which coats the future inactive X and recruits Polycomb complexes (PRC2) that lay down H3K27me3, leading to heterochromatinization and DNA methylation.

Inactivation is random in each cell and clonally maintained, producing mosaic expression. Some genes escape X-inactivation (~15-25% in humans), which contributes to sex differences in disease.

Section 02
Genomic Architecture

How genomes are organized beyond the gene level.

Human Genome Composition

~3.2 billion bp; only ~1.5% codes for protein (~20,000-25,000 genes). The rest:

Component~%Description
Introns25%Non-coding sequences within genes, removed by splicing
Intergenic DNA~25%Regulatory elements, structural, unknown function
SINEs (e.g., Alu)13%Short retrotransposons (~300 bp); ~1.1 million copies
LINEs (e.g., LINE-1)20%Long retrotransposons (~6 kb); ~500,000 copies
LTR retrotransposons8%Endogenous retroviral elements
DNA transposons3%Cut-and-paste elements (mostly inactive)
Simple repeats/satellites3%Tandem repeats, microsatellites (STRs)
Key Insight
Over 45% of the human genome derives from transposable elements. Most are inactive fossils, but some LINE-1 elements remain active and contribute to somatic mosaicism and disease.

Gene Families & Genome Duplication

Gene duplication (tandem, segmental, or whole-genome) is a major source of evolutionary novelty. Duplicated genes can diverge:

Neofunctionalization: one copy acquires a new function.
Subfunctionalization: each copy retains part of the original function.
Pseudogenization: one copy accumulates mutations and becomes a pseudogene.

Example
The globin gene family (α-globin cluster on chr 16, β-globin cluster on chr 11) arose from ancient gene duplications. Different family members are expressed at different developmental stages (embryonic → fetal → adult hemoglobin switching).

Non-Coding RNAs

The genome produces a vast repertoire of functional non-coding RNAs:

Section 03
Advanced Gene Regulation

Eukaryotic regulatory logic, enhancers, chromatin looping, and RNA-level control.

Enhancers & Chromatin Looping

Enhancers are cis-regulatory elements that can activate transcription from thousands of base pairs away (or even on different chromosomes in rare cases). They work by chromatin looping: the enhancer physically contacts the promoter through 3D genome folding, mediated by proteins like cohesin and CTCF (which defines the boundaries of topologically associating domains, TADs).

Active enhancers are marked by H3K27ac and H3K4me1. They are bound by transcription factors and the Mediator complex, which bridges enhancers to the general transcription machinery at promoters.

Disease Relevance
Disruption of TAD boundaries by structural variants can cause enhancer-gene mis-wiring, leading to developmental disorders and cancer. Many GWAS hits fall in enhancer regions, not protein-coding genes.

Post-Transcriptional Regulation

mRNA stability: AU-rich elements (AREs) in the 3' UTR target mRNAs for rapid degradation. miRNAs bind complementary sequences in the 3' UTR and recruit RISC to silence the message.

RNA editing: ADAR enzymes convert adenosine to inosine (read as guanosine), changing codon meaning post-transcriptionally. APOBEC enzymes convert cytidine to uridine.

Nonsense-mediated decay (NMD): a quality control mechanism that degrades mRNAs containing premature stop codons, preventing production of truncated proteins.

Signal Transduction & Transcription Factor Cascades

Extracellular signals ultimately regulate gene expression by activating transcription factors:

RTK/Ras/MAPK pathway: growth factor → receptor tyrosine kinase → Ras → Raf → MEK → ERK → transcription factor phosphorylation. Mutations in Ras are found in ~30% of cancers.
Wnt/β-catenin pathway: Wnt signal stabilizes β-catenin, which enters nucleus and activates TCF/LEF target genes. APC mutations (loss of β-catenin degradation) drive colorectal cancer.
p53 pathway: DNA damage activates ATM/ATR kinases → p53 stabilized → activates cell cycle arrest (p21), DNA repair, or apoptosis genes. p53 is mutated in >50% of cancers.

Section 04
Quantitative Genetics

The genetics of continuous traits: variance partitioning, heritability, and the breeder's equation.

Variance Components

For a quantitative trait, total phenotypic variance is partitioned:

VP = VG + VE + VGxE

Genetic variance can be further split: VG = VA + VD + VI

VA (additive): due to the average effects of alleles; the only component that responds to selection and is transmitted parent→offspring.
VD (dominance): due to interactions between alleles at the same locus.
VI (epistatic/interaction): due to interactions between alleles at different loci.

Heritability

Broad-sense heritability: H² = VG / VP (total genetic contribution).

Narrow-sense heritability: h² = VA / VP (additive genetic contribution). This is what matters for predicting response to selection.

Estimated by parent-offspring regression (slope = h²), twin studies (comparing MZ vs. DZ twins), or ANOVA among families.

Common Misconception
Heritability is a population-level statistic, not a measure of how much of an individual's phenotype is genetic. A heritability of 0.8 does NOT mean 80% of your height is genetic. It means 80% of the variation in height within this population is attributable to genetic differences.

The Breeder's Equation

R = h² × S

R = response to selection (change in mean of next generation). S = selection differential (difference between mean of selected parents and population mean). h² = narrow-sense heritability.

Example
A population of cattle has mean milk yield = 6,000 kg. You select cows averaging 7,500 kg to breed. S = 1,500 kg. If h² = 0.3, then R = 0.3 × 1,500 = 450 kg. The next generation's mean should be ~6,450 kg.

QTL Mapping & GWAS

Quantitative Trait Loci (QTL): genomic regions associated with variation in a quantitative trait. Identified by crossing divergent strains and correlating genotype with phenotype across markers.

Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS): scan hundreds of thousands of SNPs across a population to find loci statistically associated with a trait or disease. Most GWAS hits have small effect sizes and fall in non-coding regulatory regions.

Section 05
Advanced Molecular Mechanisms

Replication stress, recombination pathways, and RNA processing complexity.

Homologous Recombination vs. NHEJ

Double-strand breaks (DSBs) are repaired by two main pathways:

FeatureHomologous Recombination (HR)Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ)
TemplateUses sister chromatid as template (high fidelity)No template; joins broken ends directly
Cell cycleS/G2 phase (sister chromatid available)Any phase
AccuracyError-freeError-prone (can introduce indels)
Key proteinsBRCA1, BRCA2, RAD51Ku70/Ku80, DNA-PKcs, Ligase IV
Disease linkBRCA1/2 mutations → breast/ovarian cancerDefects → immunodeficiency (V(D)J recombination uses NHEJ)
CRISPR Connection
CRISPR-Cas9 makes a DSB. If the cell repairs by NHEJ → indels → gene knockout. If a donor template is provided and HR occurs → precise gene editing (knock-in).

Telomere Biology

Human telomeres consist of thousands of TTAGGG repeats. With each round of replication, the lagging strand loses ~50-200 bp (the end-replication problem) because RNA primers at the very end cannot be replaced.

Telomerase (a reverse transcriptase with an RNA template component, TERC) extends the 3' overhang. Active in germ cells, stem cells, and ~90% of cancers (enabling unlimited division). Somatic cells lack telomerase; telomere shortening acts as a mitotic clock triggering replicative senescence.

Shelterin complex (TRF1, TRF2, POT1, etc.) caps telomeres and prevents them from being recognized as DSBs.

RNA Splicing Mechanisms

The spliceosome is composed of five snRNPs (U1, U2, U4, U5, U6) plus many protein factors. Splicing proceeds via two transesterification reactions, generating a lariat intermediate.

Splice site mutations at the conserved GT (5' donor) or AG (3' acceptor) dinucleotides cause exon skipping or intron retention, often leading to disease.

Self-splicing introns: Group I (uses external G nucleotide) and Group II (uses internal adenosine, lariat intermediate; evolutionary ancestor of the spliceosome).

Section 06
Transposable Elements & Genome Evolution

Jumping genes as architects of genome structure and drivers of evolution.

Mechanisms of Transposition

DNA transposons (Class II): "cut-and-paste" via transposase. Flanked by terminal inverted repeats (TIRs). Create target site duplications (TSDs) upon insertion. Mostly inactive in humans.

Retrotransposons (Class I): "copy-and-paste" via an RNA intermediate. Two subtypes:

LTR retrotransposons: resemble retroviruses (gag, pol genes flanked by long terminal repeats). Use reverse transcriptase and integrase.
Non-LTR retrotransposons: LINEs encode their own reverse transcriptase and endonuclease. SINEs (like Alu) are non-autonomous and hijack LINE machinery for retrotransposition.

Active in Humans
LINE-1 (L1) remains retrotranspositionally active, causing ~1 in 250 disease-causing mutations. Alu elements (SINEs) continue to retrotranspose at a lower rate. New insertions cause hemophilia A, neurofibromatosis, and other disorders.

Evolutionary Impact of TEs

Transposable elements are not just "junk": they have been co-opted (exapted/domesticated) for host functions throughout evolution.

Gene regulation: TE-derived sequences serve as enhancers, promoters, and insulators. ~25% of human promoter regions contain TE-derived sequences.
New genes: RAG1/RAG2 recombinase (essential for V(D)J recombination in adaptive immunity) evolved from a DNA transposon. Syncytin (essential for placental formation) derived from an endogenous retroviral envelope gene.
Genome restructuring: ectopic recombination between dispersed repeats causes deletions, duplications, inversions, and translocations.

Section 07
Cancer Genetics

The multi-hit model, oncogenes, tumor suppressors, and genomic instability.

Oncogenes & Tumor Suppressors

Proto-oncogenes → OncogenesTumor Suppressors
Normal functionPromote cell growth/divisionInhibit growth, promote apoptosis or repair
Mutation typeGain-of-function (dominant)Loss-of-function (recessive at cell level)
Copies neededOne mutant allele sufficientBoth alleles must be inactivated (Knudson's two-hit)
ExamplesRAS, MYC, HER2, BCR-ABLTP53, RB, APC, BRCA1/2
ActivationPoint mutation, amplification, translocationDeletion, nonsense mutation, promoter methylation, LOH
Knudson's Two-Hit Hypothesis
Familial retinoblastoma: inherit one mutant RB allele (first hit), then lose the second copy somatically (second hit = LOH). Sporadic cases require both hits in the same somatic cell, explaining later onset and unilateral disease.

Hallmarks of Cancer & Genomic Instability

Cancer cells acquire capabilities including: sustained proliferative signaling, evasion of growth suppressors, resistance to apoptosis, replicative immortality (telomerase reactivation), angiogenesis, invasion/metastasis, metabolic reprogramming, and immune evasion.

Genomic instability accelerates these: microsatellite instability (MSI, from mismatch repair defects, e.g., Lynch syndrome), chromosomal instability (CIN, aneuploidy), and chromothripsis (catastrophic shattering and reassembly of a chromosome).

Section 08
Population & Evolutionary Genomics

Molecular evolution, selection signatures, and coalescent theory basics.

Molecular Evolution & Neutral Theory

Kimura's Neutral Theory: most molecular evolution is driven by genetic drift of selectively neutral mutations, not positive selection. The rate of neutral substitution equals the mutation rate (μ), independent of population size.

Molecular clock: if mutations accumulate at a roughly constant rate, sequence divergence between species estimates time since their last common ancestor. Calibrated using fossil dates.

Ka/Ks ratio (dN/dS): the ratio of non-synonymous (Ka) to synonymous (Ks) substitutions. Ka/Ks < 1 = purifying selection; Ka/Ks = 1 = neutral evolution; Ka/Ks > 1 = positive selection.

Detecting Selection in Genomes

Selective sweep: positive selection on an allele also carries nearby linked variants to high frequency, reducing local diversity. Detected by long haplotype homozygosity (EHH), high Fst, reduced heterozygosity.
Balancing selection: maintains multiple alleles, increasing heterozygosity. Example: MHC/HLA locus has extreme polymorphism maintained by pathogen-mediated selection.
Background selection: purifying selection against deleterious alleles reduces linked neutral diversity (similar pattern to sweeps but driven by negative selection).

Coalescent Theory (Basics)

Instead of thinking forward in time (population growth/drift), coalescent theory traces alleles backward to their most recent common ancestor (MRCA). In a diploid population of size N, the expected time for two alleles to coalesce is 2N generations.

This framework is the foundation of modern population genetics inference: estimating population size history, migration rates, and divergence times from DNA sequence data.

Section 09
Advanced Biotechnology

Next-gen sequencing, genome editing refinements, and functional genomics.

Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)

Illumina (sequencing by synthesis): library prep → bridge amplification on flow cell → reversible terminator chemistry → massively parallel short reads (150-300 bp). Dominant platform for most applications.

Long-read: PacBio (SMRT, single molecule) and Oxford Nanopore produce reads >10 kb, enabling resolution of structural variants, repetitive regions, and phasing of haplotypes. Higher error rate, corrected by depth or hybrid approaches.

Applications: whole genome sequencing, RNA-seq (transcriptome), ChIP-seq (protein-DNA interactions), ATAC-seq (open chromatin), bisulfite-seq (DNA methylation), single-cell sequencing.

Beyond CRISPR-Cas9

Base editors: fuse catalytically dead Cas9 (dCas9) with a deaminase to convert C→T or A→G without making a DSB. No donor template needed.

Prime editing: fuse Cas9 nickase with reverse transcriptase plus a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) that encodes the desired edit. Can make any small edit (substitutions, insertions, deletions) without DSBs or donor DNA.

CRISPRi/CRISPRa: dCas9 fused to repressor (KRAB) or activator (VP64) domains for gene silencing or activation without cutting DNA.

Advantage
Base editing and prime editing avoid the error-prone DSB repair step, reducing unwanted indels and off-target effects compared to standard Cas9.

Functional Genomics Approaches

CRISPR screens: genome-wide libraries of guide RNAs are used to knock out every gene individually, then screen for phenotypes (e.g., drug resistance). Identifies essential genes and drug targets.

RNA-seq / scRNA-seq: measures gene expression across the whole transcriptome. Single-cell RNA-seq reveals cell-type heterogeneity within tissues.

Hi-C: captures 3D genome organization (chromosome conformation capture). Reveals TADs, compartments (A/B), and long-range chromatin interactions.
